## What we know

**TCS threshold unchanged** — LRS TCS-free limit remains ₹10 lakh per financial year; it was raised from ₹7L to ₹10L in Budget 2025[^lrs-tcs-2026]
**Edu & medical TCS cut** — Outward remittances for education (without loan) and medical purposes drop from 5% to 2% TCS on amounts above ₹10L, effective April 1, 2026[^lrs-tcs-2026]
**Travel forex TCS unchanged** — Forex card purchases and general outward remittances above ₹10L still attract 20% TCS; no relief for leisure travel spends[^lrs-tcs-2026]
**Education loan exempt** — Remittances funded by education loans remain fully exempt (0% TCS) with no change[^lrs-tcs-2026]
**TCS is refundable** — TCS is adjustable against total income tax liability and claimable via ITR (Form 26AS); it is not a net cost if tax is owed[^lrs-tcs-2026]

## Regulatory Intel

**Status:** Budget 2026 proposal; TCS rate changes effective April 1, 2026. LRS threshold and general forex TCS rates unchanged.

**What it requires:** Banks, card issuers, and forex providers must collect TCS at 2% (down from 5%) on outward remittances for education and medical purposes above ₹10L. The 20% TCS on general forex purchases and non-education/medical remittances above ₹10L continues unchanged. Entities must update collection systems by April 1, 2026.

**Posture:** Neutral-to-positive for Scapia's core travel-credit-card use case. International card spends routed via the credit card network (not classified as LRS forex purchase) are typically outside LRS TCS scope — [Inferred · High] — but forex card and direct remittance products (competitors like Niyo, Wise) carry the unchanged 20% TCS exposure above ₹10L.

**Implication:** The 20% TCS on forex cards above ₹10L remains a structural competitive advantage for Scapia's zero-forex-markup credit card positioning — customers who keep annual international card spends under ₹10L (the large majority) face zero TCS either way, but high-spend travelers are pushed away from forex cards toward credit cards. Budget 2026 does not disturb this dynamic. The edu/medical TCS cut is a consumer-welfare move with limited direct impact on Scapia's product but signals continued government intent to ease LRS burdens [Inferred · Medium].

## Coverage

### Budget 2026 Eases LRS TCS
Budget 2026 delivers targeted relief on outward remittances: TCS on education and medical remittances under LRS drops from 5% to 2% above the ₹10 lakh threshold, effective April 1, 2026[^lrs-tcs-2026]. The ₹10L threshold itself is unchanged — it was already raised from ₹7L in Budget 2025[^lrs-tcs-2026]. General forex card purchases and non-education outward remittances remain at the stiff 20% TCS above ₹10L, a rate that has consistently pressured the forex-card category.

For Scapia, the status quo on travel forex TCS is quietly favorable[^lrs-tcs-2026]. International credit card spends are not classified as LRS forex purchases [Inferred · High], so Scapia's zero-markup card sidesteps the 20% TCS friction that burdens forex cards — a competitive moat the Budget leaves intact. High-volume leisure travelers rationally prefer credit cards over forex cards precisely because of this TCS asymmetry [Inferred · Medium].
