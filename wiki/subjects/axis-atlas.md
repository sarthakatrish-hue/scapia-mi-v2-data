## Competitor Intel

### Where we win
**Zero forex, zero fee** — Scapia's ₹0 annual fee and 0% forex markup contrast sharply with Atlas's ₹5,000 + GST fee and 3.5% markup — now compounded by drastically lower point redemption value post-devaluation[^atlas-apr2026]
**No partner removal risk** — Scapia's travel rewards are not contingent on third-party airline or hotel programmes that can be silently stripped; Atlas cardholders lost Accor and Marriott value overnight[^atlas-apr2026]
**No prior-notice violation** — Scapia does not implement benefit changes without advance communication; Axis's MITC-violating overnight rollout is a trust differentiator[^atlas-apr2026]
**Simpler, stable redemption** — The Atlas 2:1 ratio reversal (from 1:2) means cardholders now need twice the EDGE Miles for the same partner points — a compounding loss for those who have been accumulating[^atlas-apr2026]

### Where they're ahead
**Residual transfer ecosystem** — Even with Accor, Marriott, and Qatar removed, Atlas retains British Airways, Finnair, Vietnam Airlines, and other partners at the 2:1 ratio — still a transfer option Scapia does not offer[^atlas-apr2026]
**Established transfer ecosystem** — 20 airline and hotel partners including international heavyweights (KrisFlyer, MileagePlus, Qatar, Flying Blue) at a 1:2 ratio remains a meaningful advantage for points-optimisers[^atlas-deval1]
**Milestone bonus residual value** — Even post-devaluation, ₹15L spend still yields ₹15,000 equivalent in milestone miles, a standalone decent return for very high spenders[^atlas-deval1]
**Tiered lounge depth** — Priority Pass + DreamFolks tiered access (up to 12 intl / 18 dom at Platinum) continues to outgun most no-fee cards on raw lounge volume

### What to do
**Target stranded-point holders** — Users who accumulated EDGE Miles specifically for Accor or Marriott redemptions now hold devalued inventory; direct messaging to this cohort with Scapia's zero-complexity travel rewards[^atlas-apr2026]
**Exploit the ratio-reversal story** — The 1:2 → 2:1 change is mathematically stark (75% value loss on partner conversions); surface this in comparison content aimed at travel hobbyists who track point value[^atlas-apr2026]
**Third devaluation pattern** — Atlas has now undergone three devaluation rounds (Sep 2023, Apr 2024, Apr 2026); use the pattern in CRM and paid messaging to reinforce Scapia's stability narrative[^atlas-apr2026]

## Coverage

### Atlas Flagged in Forex Guide
A 2026 consumer guide benchmarking forex cards vs credit cards for Indian international travel explicitly names Axis Atlas as carrying 3.5% forex markup — framing it as a card with strong travel rewards but no forex-cost advantage, contrasted directly against its sibling Axis Magnus Burgundy Private at 0%[^forex-cc-2026-06-27]. The guide positions zero-markup cards as the recommended tool for frequent international travellers, structurally disadvantaging Atlas on the cost dimension even as its rewards programme remains intact.

No new product changes are evidenced; the 3.5% markup and ₹5,000 annual fee remain as previously recorded. The significance is narrative: mainstream consumer guides in 2026 are now using Atlas's forex cost as an explicit negative data point in the zero-markup vs premium-card comparison — widening the perception gap between Atlas and newer zero-cost alternatives including Scapia. ### Atlas Specs Confirmed in Roundup — 2026-06-26

A broad best-travel-cards-in-India comparison piece confirms Axis Atlas's standing fee structure: ₹5,000 annual fee, 3.5% forex markup, and income threshold of ₹12L salaried / ₹15L self-employed[^bttc-2026-06-26]. The accelerated earn rate of 5 EDGE Miles per ₹100 on travel (airlines, hotels, Travel EDGE Portal) and 2 EDGE Miles per ₹100 elsewhere is restated. No new product changes are present; existing devaluation analysis remains the primary competitive signal.

This roundup adds no material change to the April 2026 devaluation story already on record, but corroborates specs from an independent source and reinforces Atlas's position as a high-fee, high-forex card — useful context for Scapia comparison content targeting Atlas's ₹12L+ salaried audience [Inferred · Medium][^bttc-2026-06-26].

### Atlas April 2026 Partner Purge — 2026-04-02

Axis Atlas cardholders woke on 2 April 2026 to find Accor Live Limitless, Marriott Bonvoy, and Qatar Airways Privilege Club removed from the Travel Edge platform overnight — taking with them the highest-value redemption options on the card[^atlas-apr2026]. The transfer ratio simultaneously reversed from 1:2 to 2:1, meaning cardholders now need twice the EDGE Miles for the same partner points, a 75% reduction in conversion value. No 30-day notice was given, violating Axis's own MITC commitments[^atlas-apr2026].

This is Atlas's third material devaluation in three years and the most damaging for existing point-accumulators who built balances targeting specific hotel or airline programmes[^atlas-apr2026]. For Scapia, the Accor and Marriott removal is a recruitment moment: users who were holding points for a specific stay now have no path to that redemption, and the MITC-violation angle gives Scapia a concrete trust narrative beyond simple reward-rate comparison.
