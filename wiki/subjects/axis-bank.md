## What we know

**April 2026 portfolio-wide devaluation** — Axis Bank cut benefits across both cashback and travel card portfolios effective April 2026, affecting Airtel Axis, Magnus, Atlas, Horizon, and Olympus cards simultaneously[^axis-apr2026]
**No 30-day prior notice given** — Axis Bank's own MITC requires 30 days advance notice before changing benefits; the April 2 travel changes were rolled out overnight without prior communication, triggering cardholder backlash[^axis-apr2026]
**Accor, Marriott, Qatar Airways removed** — Three of the most popular transfer partners on the Travel Edge platform were removed entirely, eliminating what many users considered the highest-value redemption options[^axis-apr2026]
**Transfer ratios slashed across all travel cards** — Magnus ratio halved (5:4 → 5:2), Atlas dramatically reversed (1:2 → 2:1), Horizon halved (1:1 → 2:1), Olympus halved (1:4 → 1:2)[^axis-apr2026]
**Lounge access discontinued on Airtel Axis** — 4 complimentary domestic lounge visits per year removed entirely with no spend threshold workaround[^axis-apr2026]
**Cashback caps made spend-dependent** — Airtel Axis cashback caps converted from flat ₹250/month to a multiple of base category spend, meaning users who don't spread spend get zero incremental cashback[^axis-apr2026]
**Swiggy and BigBasket removed from Airtel Axis** — Replaced with Zomato, Blinkit, and District Movies wallet credits — partner wallet credits rather than statement cashback, a meaningful downgrade in liquidity[^axis-apr2026]

## Coverage

### Axis Bank's Sweeping April 2026 Devaluation — 2026-04-02

Axis Bank simultaneously cut benefits across its entire credit card portfolio in April 2026 — removing Accor, Marriott Bonvoy, and Qatar Airways from its Travel Edge platform, halving or worse transfer ratios on Magnus, Atlas, Horizon, and Olympus, and stripping the Airtel Axis card of its lounge access and straightforward cashback caps[^axis-apr2026]. The travel changes landed on 2 April without the 30-day prior notice Axis's own MITC requires, triggering immediate cardholder outrage on forums and social media[^axis-apr2026].

The scale and speed of these changes make this more than a routine devaluation — it is a confidence-shaking event for India's premium travel card segment. For Scapia, it is a concrete acquisition moment: users who built Axis point balances for specific Marriott or Accor redemptions now hold stranded value, and the MITC-violation angle gives Scapia a credibility contrast that goes beyond reward rates. ### Axis Quietly Tightens Reward Forfeiture — 2026-04-09

Axis Bank updated its MITC documents to impose stricter rules on unredeemed reward-point forfeiture when a card is closed, with changes effective October 2025[^axis-deval-2026-06-25]. Like HDFC and ICICI, Axis chose fragmented communication — SMS, email, and buried MITC edits — rather than a public announcement, following the industry pattern of incremental devaluation without headline disclosure.

The pattern matters for Scapia's positioning: the combination of tighter forfeiture rules and opaque communication is precisely the kind of incumbent behaviour that fuels cardholder distrust. Scapia's travel-first model, without complex multi-tier redemption conditions, becomes a cleaner alternative for users who've lost points to fine-print changes [Inferred · Medium].
