Amid growing debate over whether the Union government should reintroduce the Merchant Discount Rate (MDR) on UPI transactions, a parliamentary committee has called for its restoration, saying that "the sustained expansion of UPI requires a viable revenue mechanism."

"The objective of introduction of zero MDR was to make digital transactions affordable and widely accessible … However, the absence of MDR makes the UPI ecosystem financially unsustainable," said the Department of Financial Services (DFS) in its written submission to the Parliamentary Standing Committee on Finance.

## What has the Committee proposed?

It has asked the DFS for its opinion on whether:

- BHIM-UPI must be self-reliant and profitable rather than continue to require a Rs 2,000 crore budgetary allocation.
- The DFS could consider implementing a tiered MDR charging model. Under this proposed framework, street vendors and small businesses would remain exempt from MDR on UPI, while banks and larger entities would be required to pay a fee.

## What is MDR, and why is it important?

It's a small fee that merchants pay to banks for processing digital transactions. It helps cover the costs of maintaining the payments infrastructure, cybersecurity and compliance. In 2020, the Centre had removed MDR on UPI and RuPay debit card transactions. Since then, banks and payment aggregators have faced revenue challenges.

"By removing MDR, the Government of India has effectively destabilised a well established market norm of incentives in digital payments, and will end up creating perverse incentives in the payments ecosystem," Nikhil Pahwa, Founder of MediaNama, wrote back in 2020.

## Why bring back MDR?

According to the Committee, UPI is projected to process up to 150 billion transactions per month and add 600 million users over the next five to seven years. However, the country's digital payments ecosystem is facing major headwinds: slowing UPI growth momentum and a structural funding gap, both of which are impacting long-term investment in infrastructure, security and merchant onboarding.

"Presently, annual growth rate of UPI transaction volume is projected to decline to 25% in FY 2025-26 from 42% in FY 2024-25," said DFS.

Without MDR, adequate budgetary allocation from the Centre is essential to prevent further strain on the UPI payment ecosystem and the broader payments infrastructure. However, mere reliance on financial incentives by the government may lead to unsustainability and stifle innovation and the adoption of digital payments in underserved geographies, the government body said.

"The Committee would like to emphasise that establishing a viable revenue mechanism is critical to ensuring the UPI ecosystem achieves financial sustainability without perpetually straining the Government exchequer."

## Who actually bears the cost of running UPI?

The Reserve Bank of India (RBI) Governor Sanjay Malhotra said last year that UPI is not truly free, highlighting the underlying operational costs, even if users are not charged directly.

"There are costs, and these costs have to be paid by someone. Who pays is important but not as important as the fact that someone is footing the bill," RBI Governor had said then.

Under its zero MDR policy, the government provides financial incentives to offset some of the ecosystem's operational costs, but the allocation covers only a fraction of the estimated cost required to maintain and expand UPI services.

"Incentive support constitutes only 11% of the cost incurred by the industry and 14% of the potential MDR collected by the industry," said DFS in its latest report.

Ultimately, industry players, such as Payment Service Providers (PSPs) and banks, have to bear the brunt, as there is no direct revenue stream available to recover operational costs due to zero MDR on UPI and RuPay transactions, a PwC 2022 report says.

Loney Antony, Co-founder of Hitachi Payments, had previously said, "If there is no commercial model, they will be forced to shut down. Banks may have multiple ways to recover money from the merchants, but non-bank players do not have any other avenue than the MDR."

## How does MDR restoration impact consumers?

If the government follows in the footsteps of other jurisdictions such as the European Union and Australia, where a similar Merchant Service Charge is levied on online payments, the fee will remain on the merchant side, and consumers will not be charged directly for UPI services, Pranav Pai, Managing Partner at 3one4 Capital, told MediaNama on Monday.

"In March last year, the Payments Council of India had proposed a 0.3% MDR for large merchants. This should be a merchant cost only and not passed on as a consumer charge. The only way consumers would feel it is indirectly, if some merchants choose to reprice goods or add a surcharge where permitted," Pai said.

According to him, the return of MDR will help merchant acquirers, PSPs, payment gateways and aggregators that process large person-to-merchant (P2M) volumes scale their profit.

MediaNama queries sent to Paytm, PhonePe, Razorpay, Pine Labs, PayU and Juspay remained unanswered till the time of publication.

UPI accounts for a significant chunk of their businesses.

During the Q4 FY25 earnings call, MobiKwik co-founder Bipin Preet Singh said that MDR on UPI transactions will definitely be a new source of revenue for the fintech company, which has thus far evaded it.

## How much MDR will large merchants have to pay?

While the exact MDR is yet to be decided, the National Payments Corporation of India (NPCI) had lowered MDR rates to 0.9% of the transaction value for debit cards and 0.3% for UPI P2M transactions before completely eliminating it in FY22.

3one4 Capital's Pai argues that large merchants already pay MDR on non-RuPay debit card as well as credit card transactions, so a nominal fee on UPI will not likely have any material impact on them.

"For thin margin businesses, even a low MDR matters, so the policy has to be calibrated carefully. But for organised retail and large merchants, this is more a margin adjustment than a structural shock," he added.
