## What Is a Forex Card and Why Do Indian Travellers Need One?

A forex card — also called a travel card or prepaid travel card — is a preloaded card that lets you carry and spend foreign currency abroad without depending on cash exchange counters or paying your bank's standard international transaction fees. For Indians planning international travel, it is one of the most cost-effective and secure ways to manage money overseas.

Standard Indian debit cards charge a forex markup of 2.5% to 3.5% on every foreign transaction. On a 10-day Europe trip spending €2,000 (roughly ₹1,84,000 at current rates), that adds up to approximately ₹4,600–₹6,440 lost purely in markup fees — before you account for ATM withdrawal charges and currency conversion costs. The best forex cards eliminate or significantly reduce these charges.

This guide covers the top prepaid forex cards and zero-markup travel credit cards available for Indian residents in 2026, explains the RBI's LRS and TCS rules that apply to every forex card transaction, and tells you exactly which combination of cards works best for your travel style.

## The Three Charges That Silently Drain Your Travel Budget

Before comparing cards, understand the three cost layers that affect every Indian spending abroad:

### 1. Forex Markup

This is the extra percentage your bank adds on top of the real (interbank) exchange rate. Think of it as a silent commission on every rupee you spend internationally. Standard Indian debit cards charge 2.5%–3.5% on every foreign transaction. The best forex cards bring this down to zero.

### 2. Cross-Currency Fee

When you spend in a currency not loaded on your forex card — for example, paying in Swiss Francs when only Euros are loaded — many cards charge an additional cross-currency conversion fee of around 3.5%. Always load the currency of the country you are visiting, or choose a card that waives this fee.

### 3. Dynamic Currency Conversion (DCC)

When a hotel, restaurant, or ATM abroad asks "Do you want to pay in INR or local currency?" — always choose local currency. If you choose INR, the merchant's bank handles the conversion at rates 3%–5% worse than your own card network's rate. This is one of the costliest and most common mistakes Indian travellers make.

## RBI Rules Every Indian Must Know Before Loading a Forex Card

### The Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS)

Under the Reserve Bank of India's Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS), Indian residents can remit up to USD 2,50,000 per financial year (April–March) for permissible purposes including travel, education, and medical expenses. Funds loaded onto forex cards count as outward remittances under LRS. This limit applies per individual, per financial year, across all banks combined.

If you need to carry physical cash, note that you can carry a maximum of USD 3,000 in cash abroad. The rest of your travel budget must be on a forex card, traveller's cheque, or bank transfer.

### TCS (Tax Collected at Source) on Forex Cards

From April 1, 2025 onwards, the TCS exemption threshold has been revised upward to ₹10 lakh per financial year per individual. Here is how it works:

- **Forex card spending and loading below ₹10 lakh per year:** No TCS deducted
- **Above ₹10 lakh per year:** 20% TCS applies for general travel remittances (this is not an additional tax — it is credited to your Form 26AS and can be adjusted against your income tax liability or claimed as a refund while filing your ITR)

Education remittances (loan-financed): Significantly reduced TCS rates apply

Important: TCS applies to debit cards and forex cards. International credit card spending abroad was excluded from LRS applicability (pending further guidelines from the Finance Ministry as of 2026). This is why many frequent travellers carry both a forex card and a zero-markup credit card.

## How to Apply for a Forex Card in India: Step-by-Step

1. Choose your card based on destination, trip duration, and whether you want a bank-backed or fintech card
2. Check eligibility: You must be an Indian resident with a valid passport; most issuers also require a travel ticket or visa
3. Complete KYC: Submit PAN card, passport copy, and address proof; fintech cards like Wise and Niyo allow full video KYC online
4. Load your currency: Convert INR to your destination currency at the time of loading; sign Form A2 declaring the purpose under LRS
5. Track your LRS usage: Keep a record of all forex remittances across all banks — the ₹10 lakh TCS threshold is PAN-based, not bank-based
6. Claim TCS refund: If TCS is deducted and you have no tax liability, claim it back while filing your Income Tax Return

## Frequently Asked Questions

**01. What is the best forex card for first-time international travellers from India?**

The Wise Travel Card or the HDFC Multicurrency ForexPlus Card are both excellent starting points. Wise is better if you want purely digital onboarding and the lowest exchange rates. HDFC is ideal if you already bank with HDFC and want the backing of a major Indian bank.

**02. Is TCS charged every time I swipe my forex card abroad?**

No. TCS is collected at the time of loading money onto the forex card (at the bank or forex provider), not at every swipe. It applies when your total forex remittances in a financial year exceed ₹10 lakh. You can reclaim TCS while filing your annual income tax return.

**03. Can I use a forex card at any ATM abroad?**

Yes. Forex cards issued on the Visa or MasterCard network can be used at ATMs worldwide wherever that network is accepted. However, ATM withdrawal fees vary by card — some cards offer free withdrawals, others charge per transaction.

**04. What happens to the unused balance on my forex card after I return to India?**

Unused balance can typically be refunded to your bank account in INR at the prevailing exchange rate. You can also retain the balance for your next international trip (check your card's validity period).

**05. Should I choose to pay in INR or local currency when asked abroad?**

Always choose local currency. Paying in INR activates Dynamic Currency Conversion (DCC), where the merchant's bank converts your payment at a rate 3%–5% worse than your card's network rate.
